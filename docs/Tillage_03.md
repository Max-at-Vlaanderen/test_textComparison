Non-inversion tillage can influence the prevalence of certain weeds, pests, and diseases in agricultural systems. Here are some common considerations:
1.	**Weeds**: 
    -	Non-inversion tillage often leads to an increase in perennial weeds and those that thrive in disturbed soils. Common examples include:
        -	Creeping thistle (Cirsium arvense)
        -	Dandelion (Taraxacum officinale)
        -	Common ragweed (Ambrosia artemisiifolia)
2.	**Pests**:
    -	The soil biological quality, including the presence of beneficial arthropods, can be affected by non-inversion tillage. This practice may support a higher population of certain pests, such as:
        -	Wireworms (Agriotes spp.): These pests can thrive in undisturbed soil layers.
        -	Root maggots (Delia spp.): They may also become more prevalent due to the retention of soil organic matter.
3.	**Diseases**:
    -	Soil-borne diseases can be influenced by the management of soil soil structure and moisture retention. Non-inversion tillage may lead to:
        -	Fungal diseases: Such as root rot, which can thrive in compacted or poorly aerated soils.
        -	Bacterial diseases: Like bacterial wilt, which can be exacerbated by high moisture levels in the soil.
4.	**Soil Biological Quality**:
    -	The quality of soil biology, indicated by metrics like QBS-ar (Soil Biological Quality based on Arthropods), can improve under non-inversion tillage, promoting beneficial organisms that help suppress pests and diseases.
5.	**Soil Basal Respiration**:
    -	Non-inversion tillage can enhance soil basal soil respiration, indicating higher microbial activity. This can lead to better soil nutrient cycling but may also create conditions favorable for certain pathogens if not managed properly.
6.	**Invasive Alien Species**:
    -	The practice may inadvertently support the establishment of invasive species that can outcompete native flora, leading to ecological imbalances.

In summary, while non-inversion tillage can improve soil health and biological activity, it may also lead to challenges with specific weeds, pests, and diseases. It's essential to monitor these factors and consider integrated pest management strategies to mitigate potential issues.
